# How to Keep Track of Multiple Medications Without Losing Your Mind

*January 22, 2026*

If you take more than a couple of medications or supplements each day, you already know the problem. Different pills at different times. Some with food, some without. One that's twice a day, another that's every other day. And then someone asks "what medications are you on?" and you're scrolling through your phone trying to remember.

You're not forgetful. The system is just hard.

## Why It Gets Complicated

Most people start with one or two prescriptions and manage fine. But health routines grow. A doctor adds a new prescription. You start a supplement based on your own research. Maybe you're on TRT, insulin, or something else with its own schedule. Before long, you're juggling five, ten, even fifteen different things per day.

The mental load adds up fast. Not because any single medication is complicated, but because keeping track of all of them together is a different kind of problem.

## What Doesn't Work (For Long)

**Memory alone.** Works until it doesn't. "Did I already take that this morning?" is a question nobody should have to ask.

**Pill organizers.** Great for simple routines with pills only. They fall apart when you add injections, patches, drops, liquids, or anything that doesn't fit in a tiny plastic compartment.

**Spreadsheets.** Some people try tracking in Excel or Google Sheets. It works for about a week before the effort of updating it outweighs the benefit.

**Notes on your phone.** A static list of medications is useful, but it doesn't track what you actually took and when.

## What Actually Works

The best tracking system has a few things in common, regardless of how you set it up:

**It covers everything in one place.** Prescriptions, supplements, injections, patches, drops, powders. If it's part of your health routine, it belongs in the same system. Splitting things across multiple apps or lists is how things get missed.

**It tracks when you actually take something.** Not just what you're supposed to take, but what you did take. That log becomes incredibly valuable over time, especially when talking to your doctor.

**It handles different schedules.** Daily, twice daily, every other day, weekly, as-needed. Your system needs to be flexible enough to match your actual routine.

**It travels with you.** If your tracking system is stuck on your kitchen counter or your desktop computer, it's not there when you need it. Your phone is always with you, so that's where tracking should live too.

**It's fast.** If logging a dose takes more than a few seconds, you'll stop doing it. The best systems make it almost effortless.

## Getting Started

If you're currently tracking nothing, don't try to build the perfect system on day one. Start with the medications that matter most. Maybe that's your prescriptions, or whatever has the most complicated schedule. Get those dialed in first, then add supplements and other items over time.

The goal isn't perfection. It's having a reliable record of what you're taking and when, so that when your doctor asks, or when you're wondering whether you already took something today, you have an answer.
